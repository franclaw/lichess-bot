VARIANT 3: Describe 3 simple chess tactical or positional scenarios where the best move is absolutely obvious even to a beginner.
----------------------------------------
Here are three exceptionally simple chess scenarios where the best move is **immediately obvious to an absolute beginner**—requiring no calculation beyond spotting a direct, unstoppable threat or opportunity. Each relies on basic pattern recognition (like seeing a free piece or an inevitable mate), with **zero alternatives** that aren’t clearly worse. I’ve chosen positions where even a novice would instinctively see the solution within seconds:

---

### 1. **The Hanging Piece (En Prise)**  
   - **Scenario**:  
     White: King on e1, Queen on d4, Rook on a1, Pawns on 2nd rank.  
     Black: King on e8, Queen on a5 (undefended!), Rook on h8, Pawns on 7th rank.  
     *It’s Black’s turn to move.*  
   - **Why the move is obvious**:  
     Black’s Queen on a5 is **attacked by White’s pawn on b4** (if White just played b4-b5) or **by White’s bishop on c4** (common in openings like the Italian Game). Crucially, *no Black piece defends the a5-square*.  
     - **Best move**: **Black captures the attacking piece** (e.g., `...Qxc4` if bishop on c4, or `...Qxb4` if pawn on b4).  
     - **Why it’s obvious**:  
       - Taking the Queen gains material (a Bishop or pawn for free) with no risk.  
       - *All other moves are terrible*: Moving the Queen away loses it anyway (since it’s still attacked); doing nothing lets White win it next move; blocking isn’t possible (it’s a slider attack).  
     - **Beginner’s thought**: *"Ooh, I can take that bishop/pawn for free! Why wouldn’t I?"*  

---

### 2. **The Back-Rank Mate (With No Escape)**  
   - **Scenario**:  
     White: King on e1 (trapped by pawns on f2, g2, h2), Rook on d1, Queen on h5.  
     Black: King on e8 (trapped by pawns on f7, g7, h7), Rook on f8.  
     *It’s White’s turn to move.*  
   - **Why the move is obvious**:  
     White’s Rook on d1 controls the entire 1st rank. Black’s King is stuck on e8 because its own pawns (f7, g7, h7) block all escape squares—and **no Black piece guards the 1st rank** (e.g., no piece on d8, e8, or f8 to block).  
     - **Best move**: **White delivers checkmate with `Rd8#`**.  
     - **Why it’s obvious**:  
       - If White does *anything else* (e.g., move the Queen, push a pawn), Black escapes by playing `...Rf1+` (or similar), winning material or forcing a draw.  
       - There is **no defense** for Black: Blocking `Rd8#` is impossible (no piece can go to d8), and the King can’t move (trapped by its own pawns).  
     - **Beginner’s thought**: *"My rook can jump to d8 and check the king... and he has nowhere to go! That’s checkmate!"*  

---

### 3. **Capturing an Undefended Checking Piece**  
   - **Scenario**:  
     White: King on g1, Queen on d3, Knight on f3.  
     Black: King on g8, Bishop on h4 (checking the White King along the h4-e1 diagonal!), Rook on a8.  
     *It’s White’s turn to move (and they are in check).*  
   - **Why the move is obvious**:  
     Black’s Bishop on h4 is giving check—but it’s **undefended** (no Black piece protects h4; e.g., no pawn on g5 or knight on f5).  
     - **Best move**: **White captures the checking Bishop with `Qxh4`** (or `Nxh4` if the knight is better placed).  
     - **Why it’s obvious**:  
       - Taking the Bishop removes the check *and* gains material (a Bishop for free).  
       - *All other moves lose badly*:  
         - Blocking with `g3`? Loses the pawn after `...Bxg3` (and White’s king is still exposed).  
         - Moving the king (e.g., `Kh1`) lets Black play `...Bxg3`, winning material.  
         - Ignoring the check is illegal (you *must* get out of check).  
     - **Beginner’s thought**: *"I’m in check, but I can take that bishop! It’s not protected, so taking it fixes the check and wins a piece."*  

---

### Why These Work for Beginners  
- **No calculation needed**: Each solution relies on spotting a single, concrete pattern (free piece, trapped king, undefended checker).  
- **Zero alternatives**: Every other move is either illegal (ignoring check), immediately losing material, or leads to mate—so the "obvious" move isn’t just good; it’s the *only* reasonable choice.  
- **Builds confidence**: These are the "gifts" of chess—moves that feel like finding money on the sidewalk. Recognizing them trains beginners to prioritize safety and material first, forming the bedrock of tactical vision.  

> 💡 **Teaching tip**: Show these positions on a board and ask: *"What’s the first thing you see?"* Beginners will consistently point to the hanging piece, the back-rank vulnerability, or the undefended checker—proving how intuitive these are. Save complex forks/pins for later; these are the *absolute* starters. 🏁
