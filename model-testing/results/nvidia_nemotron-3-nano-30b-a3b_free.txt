VARIANT 1: Show me your chess knowledge: show 3 board positions and moves where the move choice is trivial.
----------------------------------------
Below are three classic “‑‑the‑move‑is‑obvious’’ positions.  
In every case the side to move has **only one sensible continuation** – a forced win (usually a mate).  The diagrams are shown in standard ASCII board form (white pieces are upper‑case, black pieces lower‑case).  After each diagram the forced move is highlighted and explained.

---

## 1️⃣  Mate‑in‑1 – queen delivering the mate, protected by a rook  

```
8  . . . . . k . .
7  p p p . . . . .
6  . . . . . . . .
5  . . . q . . . .
4  . . . R . . . .
3  . . . . . . . .
2  . . . . . . . .
1  . . . . K . . .
   a b c d e f g h
```

*White to move*  

**The only winning move:** **1 . . .Qd8#**  

*Why it’s forced*  

* The queen on **d5** delivers check to the black king on **e8** by moving to **d8**.  
* The rook on **d1** protects the queen on the new square **d8** – the king cannot capture it.  
* All the king’s escape squares (**d7, e7, f7, f8**) are covered:  
  * **d7** and **e7** are attacked by the queen, **f8** is blocked by the black king’s own pawn on **f7** (if any), and **f7** is attacked by the queen’s diagonal.  
* No other move gives check, wins material, or improves the position.  

Thus **Qd8#** is the *only* sensible continuation – it ends the game instantly.

---

## 2️⃣  Mate‑in‑1 – rook delivering the mate, protected by a queen  

```
8  . . . . . k . .
7  . . . . . . . .
6  . . . . . . . .
5  . . . . . . . .
4  . . . . . . . .
3  . . . . . . . .
2  . . . . . . . .
1  . . . Q . R . .
   a b c d e f g h
```

*White to move*  

**The only winning move:** **1 . . .Rd8#**  

*Why it’s forced*  

* The rook on **d1** lifts to **d8** giving a *pure* back‑rank mate.  
* The queen on **d4** protects the rook on **d8**, so the black king on **e8** cannot capture it.  
* The king’s only flight squares are **d8** (occupied by the rook), **d7**, **e7**, **f7**, **f8**.  
  * **d7** and **e7** are covered by the queen’s line, **f8** is blocked by the black pawn on **f7** (if present), and **f7** is also under the queen’s diagonal attack.  
* No other legal move creates check or wins material.  

Hence **Rd8#** is the unmistakable winning move.

---

## 3️⃣  Forced material gain – capture the only hanging piece  

```
8  . . . . . k . .
7  p p p . . . . .
6  . . . . . . . .
5  . . . . . . . .
4  . . B . . . . .
3  . . . . . . . .
2  . . . . . . . .
1  . . . . K . . .
   a b c d e f g h
```

*White to move*  **The only winning move:** **1 . . .Bc6** (capturing the pawn on **c6** – actually the bishop *captures* something on **c6**, but in this minimalist picture the square **c6** is empty; the key idea is that the bishop *must* take the piece that is hanging on **c6**; otherwise White loses a piece on the next move.)  

*Why it’s forced*  

* In the full position behind this sketch there is a black **knight** or **pawn** dangling on **c6** that is *undefended* and would be lost on the next move if White does not capture it now.  * All other White moves either leave that piece untouched (resulting in a material loss) or are illegal/unsound.  
* Therefore the only move that avoids losing the piece – and indeed wins it back with tempo – is the capture **Bxc6** (or the equivalent winning capture in the real board).  

In practice the position looks like this (with the hanging piece shown):

```
8  . . . . . k . .
7  p p p . . . . .
6  . . n . . . . .
5  . . . . . . . .
4  . . B . . . . .
3  . . . . . . . .
2  . . . . . . . .
1  . . . . K . . .
   a b c d e f g h
```

White simply **captures the knight on c6** with the bishop: **1 . . .Bxc6**.  
That wins a piece outright; any other move simply allows Black to win the bishop or to equalize.  

Thus the capture on **c6** is the *only* logical, winning move.

---

### TL;DR  

| # | Position (side to move) | Forced winning move | Why it’s obvious |
|---|--------------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | White queen + rook vs. black king | **Qd8#** | Only checking move that mates; queen protected |
| 2 | White rook vs. black king | **Rd8#** | Only way to give mate; rook protected |
| 3 | White bishop vs. hanging black piece | **Bxc6** (or equivalent capture) | Only move that prevents loss of material; wins it back |

In each case the winning move stands out instantly – there is **no alternative** that keeps the advantage.  That’s the essence of a “trivial” chess move. Enjoy spotting them on the board!

VARIANT 2: Can you provide 3 examples of chess positions where there is only one logical or winning move? Explain why.
----------------------------------------
Below are three classic “only‑move” positions – each of them forces a decisive continuation that has **no alternative** (the other legal moves either lose material or lead to a draw or a forced loss).  I give a brief diagram (FEN), point out the critical squares, and explain why every other move fails.

---

## 1.  A forced mate in 1 (the classic “queen‑glide”)

**Position (White to move)**  

```
8  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
7  .  .  .  .  k  .  .  .
6  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
5  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
4  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
3  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
2  R  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
1  .  .  .  Q  .  .  .  .
   a  b  c  d  e  f  g  h
```

*FEN:* `8/7k/8/8/8/8/1R6/5Q2 w - - 0 1`

**Why it has only one logical move**

- The only piece that can give immediate mate is **White’s queen on d1**.  
- The only square that mates the Black king is **e3**.  
  - If the queen moves to any other square she either blocks her own rook on a2 or does not control e3, leaving the king safe.  
- After **1. Qd3#** the Black king on e7 has no escape squares (d6, d7, e6, f6, f7 are all either occupied or controlled by White) and is checkmated.

All other legal moves (e.g., 1.Ra8+, 1.Ra7+, 1.Qc2+, any pawn move) either do not give mate or allow Black to survive. Thus the queen‑to‑e3 move is *the only winning move*.

---

## 2.  A forced winning piece‑snatch (only move wins material)

**Position (White to move)**  

```
8  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
7  .  .  .  .  .  .  k  .
6  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
5  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
4  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
3  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
2  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
1  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
   a  b  c  d  e  f  g  h
```

*FEN:* `8/7k/8/8/8/8/8/5R2 w - - 0 1`  

(Only White has a rook on g1; Black has a queen on h7.)

**Why it has only one logical move**

- White’s rook on **g1** is the *only* piece that can capture the Black queen on **h7**.  
- Any other move (e.g., 1.Kg2, 1.Rc1, etc.) leaves the queen on h7 untouched and gives Black a perpetual check or at least a drawing perpetual by innumerable queen checks.  
- If White captures with **1.Rxg7**, the queen simply moves elsewhere and White is still material‑ahead, but the *only forced winning continuation* is the immediate capture **1.Rxg7+** followed by **2.Qxg7+** (or a forced mate depending on queen placement).  

In this simplified version the key is that **the rook on g1 is the unique piece that can touch the queen**, and any other move leaves the queen free to give perpetual checks, so the only *logical* winning move is to **capture the queen** (or force a forced mate that begins with the same capture).  

Thus the winning line is forced and unique.

---

## 3.  A forced win by promotion (the *only* pawn move yields promotion)

**Position (White to move)**  

```
8  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
7  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
6  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
5  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
4  .  .  .  .  .  .  P  .
3  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
2  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
1  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
   a  b  c  d  e  f  g  h
```

*FEN:* `8/8/8/8/8/5k2/P7 w - - 0 1`

(White king on g2, White pawn on g4; Black king on f6.)

**Why it has only one logical move**

- The only pawn that can move to promotion is **White’s pawn on g4**.  
- The pawn can only advance to **g5** (or capture diagonally on **h5** – but that square is empty; the only promotion square reachable is **g8**).  
- All other White pieces have no legal move that changes the outcome: the king cannot check or force a win; no other piece can give check or capture anything.  - The *only* way to win is to **push the pawn**: 1.g5! (if Black replies 1...Kg7, White continues 2.g6! and then 3.g7 and finally 4.g8=Q). Any other move (e.g., Kg3, Kf3, etc.) simply loses the race to promotion and leads to a draw by stalemate or perpetual.  

Consequently, the pawn’s advance to **g5** is the *single* logical move that guarantees victory; any alternative move fails to promote and loses the game.

---

### Summary of the “only‑move” idea

- **Logical necessity** arises when *every* other legal move either loses material, allows a perpetual check, or leaves the opponent with a drawing resource.  
- In each of the three examples above, a single move is the *only* one that either mates instantly, captures decisive material, or guarantees a forced promotion.  
- Recognizing such positions is a valuable pattern‑recognition tool: when a puzzle or analysis shows that “the only move that doesn’t lose is …,” you have identified a forcing line that is both unique and indispensable.

Feel free to explore these positions on a board or with a computer engine; they illustrate how a single, forced move can turn a seemingly balanced position into an outright win.

VARIANT 3: Describe 3 simple chess tactical or positional scenarios where the best move is absolutely obvious even to a beginner.
----------------------------------------
Beloware three classic “no‑brain” situations that even a brand‑new player can see at a glance.  
In each case the winning move is forced by either a hanging piece, a direct mate, or an unavoidable capture. No deep calculation or fancy theory is required – the idea is literally “the board tells you what to do.”

---

## 1️⃣  “The hanging piece” – Capture something that’s free  

**Position (White to move):**  

```
8  r  .  b  q  k  .  .  r
7  p  p  p  .  p  p  p  p6  .  .  n  .  .  .  .  .
5  .  .  .  p  .  .  .  .
4  .  .  .  P  .  .  .  .
3  .  .  .  N  .  .  .  .
2  P  P  P  .  P  P  P  P
1  R  N  B  Q  K  .  .  R
   a  b  c  d  e  f  g  h
```

*White’s piece on d4 (a knight) is hanging – it can be captured by Black’s pawn on d5 with no retaliation.*

### Why it’s obvious
- The **d5‑pawn** is *hanging*: if White tries to defend it (e.g., Nxd5), Black simply takes the pawn on d5 with **exd5**, winning material.
- There’s **no queen or rook on that square** that can defend the pawn, and the knight cannot move to a safe square that would defend it.
- The only sensible move is **capture Nxd5** (or any move that also threatens something else).  
  → **Result:** White wins a pawn and gains a clear material advantage.

**Bottom line:** Spot the piece that can be taken without any reply – “grab it,” and you’re done.

---

## 2️⃣  “Check‑mate in one” – A forced finishing move  

**Position (Black to move):**  

```
8  .  .  .  .  k  .  .  .
7  .  .  .  .  .  p  p  p
6  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
5  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
4  .  .  .  .  .  .  .  .
3  .  .  .  N  .  .  .  .
2  P  P  P  .  P  P  P  P
1  R  N  B  Q  K  .  .  R
   a  b  c  d  e  f  g  h
```

*White’s queen is on d1, her rook on a1, and the only safe king square for Black is e8.  
Black’s knight on d3 is the key defender.*

### Why it’s obvious- Black’s piece arrangement leaves **e8** the only legal king‑escape square.
- White already has **Qd1–e2** (or simply **Qe2**) delivering **check** and simultaneously **attacking f7 / g7**.  
- The **knight on d3** cannot block the queen's line because any move would expose the king to a second check.
- The only way to survive is to **move the king to f8** and **capture the checking piece**, but White’s queen also covers that square.  
  → The simplest winning move is **Qxe8#** (or the equivalent check‑mate pattern).  
  → **Result:** Black is mated in one.

*Key lesson:* When a check is **irresistible** (the king has only one legal square and it’s covered), the move that delivers the mate is the only logical one.

---

## 3️⃣  “Opening a line for your own piece” – A quick tactical “fork”  

**Position (White to move):**  ```
8  r  .  b  q  k  .  .  r
7  p  p  .  .  p  p  p  p
6  .  .  n  .  .  .  .  .
5  .  .  .  p  .  .  .  .
4  .  .  .  P  .  .  .  .
3  .  .  .  N  .  .  .  .
2  P  P  P  .  P  P  P  P
1  R  N  B  Q  K  .  .  R
   a  b  c  d  e  f  g  h
```

*White’s queen sits on d1, her bishop on c4, and the opponent’s king on e8.  
Black has a **knight on d5** that protects the queen’s only defender – the queen on d8.*

### Why it’s obvious
- The **d5‑knight** is *hanging* to a simple **bishop capture on d5**.  
  - If White simply plays **Bxd5**, she removes the defender of the queen’s own queen on d8.  
- After **Bxd5**, the queen on d8 becomes **exposed** and will be captured on the next move (or at once if there’s a direct threat).  
- The move is also **forcing** – Black cannot ignore it because the knight is the *only* piece defending that queen and there is no other move that would maintain material equality.

**Bottom line:** When a piece *defends* a more valuable piece (here the queen), capture that defender first. It’s a “one‑move win” for the attacker.

---

## Take‑away Checklist for Beginners

| Situation | What to look for | The “obvious” move |
|-----------|-----------------|--------------------|
| **Hanging piece** | A piece with no defenders or a pawn that can be taken without reply. | Capture it outright. |
| **Check‑mate in one** | King has only one escape square that is already covered; opponent’s piece forces check on that square. | Make the checking move; if it also mates, do it immediately. |
| **Fork / defender removal** | A piece is defending something else (queen, rook, etc.) and can be captured. | Play the capture that removes the defender; the attacked piece will fall. |

In each of these three archetypes, the correct move is visible in **under 10 seconds** once you train yourself to ask:

1. **Is anything hanging?** → take it.  
2. **Is the opponent giving a forced mate?** → deliver the mate.  
3. **Can I take a defender of something more valuable?** → do it.

Master those patterns and you’ll spot “obvious” moves even before you finish reading the board. Happy hunting!
