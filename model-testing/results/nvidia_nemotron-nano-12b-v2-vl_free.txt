VARIANT 3: Describe 3 simple chess tactical or positional scenarios where the best move is absolutely obvious even to a beginner.
----------------------------------------
Here are three simple chess tactical/positional scenarios where the best move is immediately obvious, even to a beginner:

---

### **1. Knight Fork**  
**Position:**  
- **White**: Knight on f3.  
- **Black**: Queen on d5, Bishop on c6 (both undefended).  

**Best Move: `Nc5`**  
This knight move forks the queen (d5) and bishop (c6), both of which are undefended and of high value. Black loses material regardless of their response. A beginner would recognize the knight’s ability to threaten two pieces at once.

---

### **2. Bishop Pin**  
**Position:**  
- **Black**: Knight on c6 (defending a queen on d5).  
- **White**: Bishop on a1 (unused).  

**Best Move: `Bc4`**  
The bishop pins the knight (c6) to the queen (d5), immobilizing the knight. Black must move the queen, allowing White to recapture it with the bishop. The student would see the bishop threatens the knight, which is more valuable than the pawn-like bishop on c6.

---

### **3. Discovered Check**  
**Position:**  
- **White**: Rook on a1, Bishop on f3.  
- **Black**: Pawn on c6 blocking the bishop’s diagonal, King on c7.  

**Best Move: `Bd2`**  
Moving the bishop exposes the rook’s diagonal, delivering a **discovered check** to the Black king (rook attacks on a7). The student would notice that advancing the bishop uncovers a checkmate threat (king must move, and White’s rook can then take the undefended pawn on c6).

---

### Summary:  
These scenarios rely on **core chess patterns**—forks, pins, and discovered attacks—that beginners can recognize quickly with minimal calculation. Each move delivers immediate tactical advantage by exploiting piece values or positional weaknesses.
